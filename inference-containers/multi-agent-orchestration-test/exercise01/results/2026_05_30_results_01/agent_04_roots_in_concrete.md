# Roots in Concrete

## I

The forest had been swallowing the world for two centuries when Elara found the door.

She was tracking a new strain of mycelium — something she'd named *Velvet Thread*, for the way it gilded rotting timber in silver threads that pulsed faintly in moonlight — when her boot struck metal. Not rusted, not corroded, but solid and cold beneath layers of leaf litter and tangled root. She knelt, brushing away centuries of accumulated earth with bare fingers, and felt the shape of a seam against her palm.

"Ren," she called back over her shoulder. "Come here."

Her brother climbed down from the ridge line like something that had been bred to it. He moved through trees the way fish move through water — not fighting the current but reading it, adjusting mid-stride for branch and root without ever looking at where his feet fell. A broad-brimmed hat, patched a dozen times with bark cloth, shaded eyes the color of wet slate.

"What is it?"

She pointed. He crouched beside her and ran his hands over the surface they'd uncovered together — flat panels of something smooth as glass but harder than stone, joined by seams so precise they seemed drawn by an instrument rather than manufactured. Her brother's fingers traced a symbol near the edge: a circle with three radiating lines, etched deep enough to have survived two hundred years of damp and root pressure.

"My grandmother told a story about these," Ren said quietly. "The sealed rooms. The ones the Before People buried before they stopped coming."

Elara didn't answer. She was looking at the panel arrangement — six squares, each roughly arm's span, arranged in a rectangle that matched no building style she'd ever seen or studied. Where wood had given way to living stone and woven wall, where shelter meant what you could make your hands build from fallen branches and mud brick, this was something else entirely. Something manufactured with an indifference to materials she couldn't quite parse.

She pressed her palm flat against the nearest seam. It was warm.

Not sun-warmed, not geothermally warmed — it held a low, steady heat, like the warmth of something sleeping beneath soil. She pressed harder, and the seam gave a barely perceptible vibration, a hum so faint she might have imagined it if her brother's face hadn't gone white beside her.

"It's still running," Ren whispered. "After all this time, it's still *running*."

They worked at it for three hours that morning, clearing earth and root and the patient work of reforestation until they had enough surface to stand back and see the door in full: a rectangle set flush into a slope beneath ancient oak, its panels dark as obsidian. And there was something beside it — a panel with symbols that matched nothing on either of their tongues, and a series of raised shapes arranged in a pattern Elara's grandmother might have recognized from storybooks: a keyboard layout.

Ren pressed the first key he could identify, then another, until by accident or intuition he hit a sequence that made the door shudder. Light poured from the seams — white, bright, wrong against the dappled green of the forest afternoon. The hum deepened into something audible, and then the panels shifted with a sound like a great inhaling, sliding open to reveal a corridor lit by strips of cold fire set into the walls.

They descended.

---

The vault was not a library.

Elara had expected one. Every elder in her settlement spoke of the Before People's obsession with storage — how they had accumulated knowledge for centuries, stacking it into towers of paper and symbols until it overflowed and burned. The elders called this their original sin: that they had hoarded understanding without learning from it, filled rooms with records of the world while the world outside those walls drowned in what they'd made.

But the vault held no books. No scrolls. No written records of any kind.

Instead, it held rows upon rows of cylinders — hundreds of them, thousands maybe, each one no taller than her forearm and encased in a material that looked like glass but felt like metal when she ran a finger along its length. Each cylinder bore a label, printed in that same alien alphabet that Ren had managed to partially decode using fragments his grandmother had preserved in memory songs.

"*Seed bank,*" he said, reading aloud from what he could reconstruct. "*Cryo-preserved. Genetic archive.*"

He turned one of the cylinders gently, and through its translucent wall she could see something inside — not data, not words, but a mass of tiny dark particles suspended in some gel-like substance. Seeds. Hundreds of types, each carefully catalogued and preserved at temperatures below freezing.

"My grandmother's story was wrong," Elara said. "They didn't store their knowledge. They stored their *food*."

"Or what would have been food," Ren corrected gently. He ran his hand along one cylinder — it was cold to the touch, frosted with rime at the edges. "Every kind of plant they could think of. Crops that grew in soil we don't have anymore. Fruits we've only heard in songs."

Elara moved down the rows and found a display panel near the end — a flat screen, dark for now but lit by faint standby indicators that still functioned after all these decades. She pressed what looked like a power symbol. The screen flickered to life with a burst of static, then resolved into text she couldn't read at all.

Ren was already there, his fingers moving across the keyboard below it with growing confidence. He'd been teaching himself the old symbols for years — not from books but from memory songs, those long rhythmic verses that every child in their settlement learned as a kind of cultural anchor. They weren't designed to teach language; they were designed to preserve fragments of it. Enough to recognize patterns. Enough, apparently, for Ren to type something that made the screen change.

The display now showed an image: a tree, tall and thick with green leaves, bearing fruit in clusters of red and yellow. Beneath it, text — but this time, slowly, Elara could read it. The words had shifted into something almost recognizable, as if the system was adjusting to her brother's inputs and learning what language he might understand.

*Malus domestica.* Apple. Common apple. 47 cultivars preserved.

She looked up at the ceiling of the vault — smooth, seamless, humming with the quiet work of systems designed for eternity — and then back down at the rows. Each cylinder was a species. Some had dozens of varieties inside; others held only one or two, preserved against some catastrophe that hadn't been catastrophic enough to kill them entirely but had changed everything regardless.

"What do we do with this?" Ren asked.

She didn't answer right away. She walked down the aisles and let her eyes move across labels — *Zea mays* (corn, forty varieties), *Solanum lycopersicum* (tomato, twenty-three), *Vitis vinifera* (grape, sixty-eight), species she'd never seen, names that meant nothing to her. The old world had been so careful, so thorough. They had frozen every thing they thought might matter.

"What do we think this means?" Ren pressed. "We could replicate all of it. Grow everything they saved."

"And what good would that do us?"

---

They spent three days in the vault before returning to their settlement with a single cylinder each — apples for Elara, tomatoes for Ren — and nothing else. They said nothing about what they'd found while they carried the cylinders home through forest and streambed, careful not to jostle them. The old machines were still running, powered by some buried source neither could identify, and they trusted that silence more than they trusted noise.

The settlement was built into a valley between two hills that had once been parking lots. Elara knew this because she'd found the painted lines — ghostly white rectangles barely visible beneath moss and root — on a survey trip five years earlier. Now the rectangle-grid gave way to something organic: houses woven from living trees, their walls formed by encouraging banyan and ivy to grow into shelterable shapes; bridges of woven reed across the creek that cut through the valley center; gardens planted not in rows but in spirals, following soil conditions rather than human convenience.

Their grandmother, Amara, received them at the settlement's central gathering space — a clearing beneath a canopy where sunlight fell through in columns and dust motes danced like small creatures. She was old enough to remember fragments of stories told by people who had been alive when the Before People stopped coming. Her hair was entirely white, her hands mapped with cracks that no oil could smooth, and her eyes were sharp as ever.

"You found something," she said, before either of them could speak.

Elara set down her cylinder. "A seed bank. Thousands of species. The old world's crops."

Amara touched the glass surface through her cracked fingers and said nothing for a long while. When she spoke, her voice was softer than Elara remembered it — not weaker, but weighted with something that had been held back for too long.

"The elders argue about this all the time," Amara said. "What to keep from what came before. What to discard. We have songs of their machines and their towers, their lights and their medicines. We have fragments of their language preserved in memory verses. We use what we need — metal tools when metal is found, fire-making from flint because it works — but we don't rebuild them. We've chosen not to."

"Why?" Elara asked.

"Because rebuilding isn't the same as learning," Amara said simply. "Look at your world, Elara. Really look at it."

She gestured toward the valley walls. The settlement clung to them like lichen — small, adapted, working within what was available rather than demanding what wasn't. There were no roads except paths worn by feet. No fences except living barriers of thorn and briar that kept livestock in without confining it. People moved through their world slowly, carefully, with the kind of attention that comes from knowing a single mistake can cost more than they can afford to lose.

"The Before People had everything this vault preserved," Amara said. "Every seed. Every variety. And they grew them all at once, everywhere at once, on land that could barely hold it. They made monocultures of the earth — vast stretches of nothing but corn, nothing but wheat, nothing but soy — because it was efficient. Because efficiency is a kind of faith: if you can't imagine what tomorrow looks like, you optimize today."

"Efficiency killed them?" Ren asked, though he'd heard the argument before.

"Efficiency blinded them," Amara said. "They couldn't see that a system which produces more and requires less is a system that doesn't need its people. They automated away their connection to the ground. And when the ground finally pushed back — droughts, blights, soil exhaustion that no amount of chemical input could mask — they had nothing in them that knew how to respond."

The old woman sat down on a woven mat and pulled her shawl tighter. "You have two choices, children. Not three. Two. You can replicate what they did — grow their seeds, feed the world with their methods, recreate their abundance — and within a generation you'll be asking yourselves the same questions we've been asking for two hundred years. Or you can take these seeds and do something different with them."

"Like what?" Elara asked.

"Like what you're already doing," Amara said, looking at her granddaughter's hands — stained green from soil, calloused from vine work, marked by a small cut she'd gotten harvesting wild berries that morning. "You study the forest. You understand it. You know which plants grow well together and which fight for space. You know how the mycelial networks connect roots and share nutrients underground. You know things the Before People never learned because they were too busy reading about them in books."

---

Elara returned to the vault alone three weeks later, carrying nothing but a satchel of her own making — stitched from cured bark with sinew thread, holding soil samples from their settlement's gardens and jars of spores from the forest floor. She needed time to think, and Ren understood enough about her work not to press.

Inside the vault, the air was cold and still. The cylinders hummed quietly around her, each one a preserved moment in botanical history. She moved through them slowly this time, reading labels with more attention than before. She wasn't looking for what was familiar — corn, wheat, rice, the staples that had fed millions — but for something else. Something the Before People had saved without knowing why it mattered yet.

She found it in a section marked *undetermined ecological function*.

These were species with no agricultural record. No yield data. No notes on caloric output or pest resistance or storage life. Just names and collection dates, scattered across what must have been hundreds of research stations before the vault was sealed. Some of them had only one specimen — a single seed or spore preserved against a future that never looked back with gratitude.

Elara sat on the floor of the vault between rows of unknowns and took out her soil samples. She spread them across her lap — dark volcanic soil from their garden, red clay from the creek bed, sandy loam from the hillside where she'd found *Velvet Thread* — and began to work without a plan.

The idea came to her like mycelial growth itself: slow, branching, connecting things that hadn't been connected before. The Before People had thought about plants individually. Each species was a variable in an equation — how much would it produce? How much would it need? What could be done with it? They'd reduced the world to inputs and outputs and called it science.

But what if they'd gotten the question wrong?

What if the problem wasn't which plant produced the most but which *combination* of plants held together the best — not just ecologically, but culturally? Not just in soil but in human lives?

She opened the cylinder marked *Arctostaphylos uva-ursi* — bearberry, a ground-cover shrub with small red berries. Then another: *Polygonum aviculare*, knotweed, which any field hand knew as a weed that grew everywhere. And then something she'd never heard of: *Crepidotus mollis*, a fungus whose only listed property was *symbiotic nitrogen fixation in coniferous root systems*.

Alone, none of these would have fed anyone. Together — and she could picture the arrangement even now, before planting a single seed — they might do something that hadn't been done in two hundred years: create a landscape that wasn't just productive but *resilient*. Not an engineered monoculture that required constant human maintenance, but a living system that maintained itself.

She worked through the vault like that for six hours — not selecting crops, not planning gardens, but assembling relationships between organisms that could survive together and thrive together, each one supporting the others in ways neither she nor the Before People had fully understood. Bearberry fixing nitrogen at high elevations where nothing else grew well. Knotweed holding soil on slopes while its roots broke up compaction for deeper-rooted species beneath. Fungi connecting it all underground, moving nutrients from abundance to scarcity like invisible roads between settlements.

By the time she left the vault with seven cylinders carefully wrapped in her satchel and tucked against her body, she understood something that had been eluding her since the door first opened: the vault wasn't a gift of abundance. It was a test.

The Before People had saved everything they could think of — every seed, every variety, every strain — because they couldn't bear to lose it. They'd frozen their world in glass and ice and waited for someone to press start. But pressing start wasn't the same as starting differently.

---

Spring came to the valley like a decision made after too much deliberation — sudden, warm, decisive in a way that took Elara by surprise. She'd been waiting for conditions to be perfect, for soil temperatures and moisture levels to reach thresholds she could measure, but the forest didn't wait for measurements. It waited for nothing at all.

She planted on a Tuesday morning, with Ren beside her and three other gardeners from their settlement — people who understood the difference between cultivating something and commanding it. The seven species Elara had chosen from the vault went into the ground in an arrangement she'd mapped out over several sleepless nights: not rows or grids but circles nested within circles, each ring supporting the next with shared resources and complementary needs.

The outer ring was bearberry — hardy, low-growing, able to establish itself where nothing else could yet reach. Inside that, knotweed, which would hold soil while building organic matter for what came next. In the center she placed the unknown fungus, its spores distributed through inoculated wood chips she'd prepared from fallen oak branches. And between each ring, carefully, she introduced species from their existing gardens — heirloom beans saved from a grandmother's seeds, wild rice collected from the creek bed, bitter herbs that no one wanted to eat but everyone needed for medicine.

It was not efficiency. It was something else — messier, slower, harder to measure in any way that would have satisfied the Before People's need for data. But it was alive in a way that felt different to her hands. When she pressed soil around the first bearberry seedling, the ground pushed back with warmth and moisture in proportions that suggested not resistance but welcome.

Three months later, Ren came to find her at the edge of their experiment. He had news — Amara was sick, something sudden and sharp that had taken hold over a single night. But he also had something else, and he stopped walking before they reached the settlement because his voice cracked around it.

"Elara," he said. "Come here."

She followed him to a point she hadn't visited since planting — the center ring of her experiment, where the fungus was supposed to be working underground, invisible, slow. But something was wrong and right at the same time: the soil had cracked in places, not from drought but from growth beneath it. Mycelial threads — thick, white, and luminous in the afternoon light — had reached the surface and spread across the ground like veins carrying life above where life itself couldn't yet be seen.

And growing among them were things she hadn't planted. Things that shouldn't have grown there. Small green shoots pushing through mycelial mats, bearing tiny leaves that looked like nothing in her grandmother's seed catalogues. Hybrid offspring of species separated in the vault by decades and continents, brought together underground by fungal networks that didn't care about human geography or agricultural records or any of the categories the Before People had used to organize a world they could no longer hold.

Ren pointed at one particular shoot — it had flowers already, tiny blue things opening in the late morning light. "That's not any plant I've ever seen."

Elara knelt and touched the leaves with two fingers. They were rough but soft, covered in minute hairs that caught moisture from air still cool after a night of rain. The shoot was no taller than her hand, fragile and impossible and absolutely real.

"The vault," she said slowly, "gave us seeds. But it can't grow them."

"No," Ren said. He looked around the clearing at the rings and the mycelial threads and the unexpected plants growing between lines that hadn't been drawn on any map. "We grow them. And we grow something else along with them."

---

Amara survived, barely, carried through a fever by herbs her granddaughter mixed from their garden stores. But she was changed — not physically, but in the way old people change when they realize that what they've been guarding all along isn't something fragile at all but something that has always been waiting for the right conditions to grow.

She came to the experiment site on a morning warm enough to take her shawl off and sat among the mycelial threads with both of her grandchildren beside her. She looked at what Elara had planted — the careful circles, the nested rings, the unexpected hybrids pushing through soil that held them more gently than any pot or planter — and she smiled in a way Elara hadn't seen in years.

"You understand now," Amara said, not as a question but as something confirmed after long waiting. "The Before People didn't collapse because they were evil. They collapsed because they forgot what connection means. They thought they could control the world by storing it — freezing it, preserving it, cataloguing it. But a seed in ice is not a seed at all. It's just a memory of one."

She reached out and touched a hybrid leaf — rough and soft and impossibly new — and closed her eyes. "Your mother would have loved this."

Elara looked away quickly because the ground was suddenly too hard to stand on and Ren put his hand on her shoulder in the way brothers do when sisters need it. Their mother had died before Elara could remember — a winter illness that took three people from their settlement in one season, before anyone knew how the old medicines worked or didn't.

"We don't replicate," Elara said to no one in particular but also to everyone who would hear her say it later. "We grow."

And then she stood up and walked back to the garden because there was work to do — always work, never urgent, always necessary — and the vault beneath the hill waited with its thousands of frozen seeds for whoever came next to press start on something entirely different.
